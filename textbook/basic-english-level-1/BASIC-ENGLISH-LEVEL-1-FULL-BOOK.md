## Preface

Welcome to **Basic English Level 1: Survival English**, the first book in the SomaliKing College (SKC) English language series.

This book was created for one reason: to help adult Somali learners speak, read, and write real English from the very first day of class. Many students who begin this course have never studied English before. Some have never held a pen in a classroom. This book was written with that learner in mind — simple language, large pictures, short sentences, and steady repetition.

Every unit in this book connects to real Somali life: greeting a neighbor, introducing your family, buying vegetables at the market, telling a taxi driver where to go, filling out a form at a government office, or answering the phone. These are not abstract grammar exercises. They are survival skills — the English a person needs to work, study, and live with confidence.

The SKC Curriculum Development Team designed this book around four goals:

1. **Simplicity** — Pre-A1 level language that never overwhelms a true beginner.
2. **Relevance** — Somali names, Somali settings, and Somali cultural context in every unit.
3. **Practice** — Every new word and structure is practiced through speaking, listening, reading, and writing.
4. **Confidence** — Small, achievable steps that let students feel success every day.

We are proud to bring this original textbook to SKC classrooms. We hope it helps every student build a foundation for a better future.

**— SKC Curriculum Development Team**

## 📐 Grammar Point: Greetings and Politeness Words

In English, we choose greetings based on the **time of day**:

| Time | Greeting |
|---|---|
| Morning (before 12 PM) | Good morning |
| Afternoon (12 PM–6 PM) | Good afternoon |
| Evening (after 6 PM) | Good evening |
| Bedtime | Good night |

We use **"Hello"** and **"Hi"** at any time of day.

We use polite words to be kind:
- **Please** — when we ask for something
- **Thank you** — when someone helps us
- **You're welcome** — when someone thanks us
- **Sorry** — when we make a mistake
- **Excuse me** — when we need attention or must pass by someone

## 🔊 Pronunciation

Listen to your teacher and repeat.

| Sound Focus | Words |
|---|---|
| /h/ sound | hello, hi, how |
| Long "oo" in "good" | good morning, good afternoon, good evening |
| Silent stress on "you" | thank you, see you |

**Practice Drill:**
Teacher says → Students repeat:
"Hello. Hello. Hello, how are you?"
"Good morning. Good morning. Good morning, teacher."
"Thank you. Thank you. Thank you very much."

**Tip:** In English, we often "link" words together smoothly: "Thankyou" sounds like one word, not two separate words.

## 📐 Grammar Point: "I am" + Personal Information

We use **"I am"** to talk about ourselves:

- I am **Amina**. *(name)*
- I am **22 years old**. *(age)*
- I am **from Somalia**. *(country)*
- I am **a student**. *(job)*
- I am **single**. *(status)*

We use question words to ask others:

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| What is your name? | My name is... |
| How old are you? | I am... years old. |
| Where are you from? | I am from... |
| What is your job? | I am a/an... |

## 📐 Grammar Point: "How many" + Plural Nouns

We use **"How many"** to ask about quantity. The noun after "how many" is usually **plural** (add -s).

- How many **pens** are there? — There are five **pens**.
- How many **students** are in class? — There are twenty **students**.

# GRAMMAR REFERENCE

[Illustration: A neat reference chart-style illustration showing a ruler, a pencil, and grammar symbols (a plus sign, a question mark) arranged around an open notebook page, teal and gold accents.]

This section brings together every grammar point taught in **Basic English Level 1**, unit by unit, for quick review.

---

## 1. The Verb "To Be" *(Units 1, 2, 6)*

| Subject | Be Verb | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I | am | I am a student. |
| You | are | You are my friend. |
| He | is | He is a teacher. |
| She | is | She is my sister. |
| It | is | It is a book. |
| We | are | We are from Somalia. |
| They | are | They are my classmates. |

---

## 2. Personal Pronouns *(Units 1, 2, 6)*

Subject pronouns: I, you, he, she, it, we, they
Possessive words: my, your, his, her, its, our, their

Example: **My** name is Amina. **Her** brother is a driver.

---

## 3. Articles: "A" and "An" *(Unit 3, 14)*

Use **"a"** before a consonant sound: a book, a pen, a teacher.
Use **"an"** before a vowel sound: an apple, an egg, an engineer.

---

## 4. This / That / These / Those *(Unit 5)*

| Word | Distance | Number |
|---|---|---|
| this | near | one |
| that | far | one |
| these | near | many |
| those | far | many |

---

## 5. Plural Nouns *(Unit 4, 5)*

Most nouns add **-s** to become plural: book → books, chair → chairs.
Some nouns change form completely (see Irregular Words section): child → children, man → men.

---

## 6. Question Words *(Units 2, 4, 5, 10, 13, 15)*

| Question Word | Used For | Example |
|---|---|---|
| What | things, names | What is your name? |
| Where | places | Where are you from? |
| When | time | When is your class? |
| How | manner | How are you? |
| How old | age | How old are you? |
| How much | price / uncountable amount | How much is this? |
| How many | countable amount | How many books? |
| Who | people | Who is your teacher? |
| Why | reason | Why are you late? |

---

## 7. Simple Present Tense *(Unit 12, 14)*

Used for routines, facts, and jobs.

| Subject | Verb Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I / You / We / They | base verb | I study English. |
| He / She / It | verb + s/es | She studies English. He teaches math. |

---

## 8. There Is / There Are *(Unit 11)*

- **There is** + singular noun: There is a bed in my room.
- **There are** + plural noun: There are two chairs.

---

## 9. Prepositions of Place *(Unit 13)*

near · next to · between · in front of · behind

Example: The clinic is **next to** the school. The bank is **between** the shop and the mosque.

---

## 10. Prepositions of Time: "On" and "In" *(Unit 9)*

- Use **on** with days: on Monday.
- Use **in** with months and years: in July, in 2026.

---

## 11. Countable and Uncountable Nouns *(Unit 16)*

- **Countable** nouns can be counted: apples, tomatoes, eggs → *How many?*
- **Uncountable** nouns cannot be counted directly: rice, water, sugar → *How much?*

---

## 12. "By" + Transport *(Unit 17)*

Use **by** to describe a method of travel: by bus, by car, by boat.
Exception: **on foot** or **I walk** (not "by walk").

---

## 13. Polite Requests and Responses *(Unit 1, 8)*

- **I want** (direct) vs. **I would like** (polite): "I would like some tea, please."
- Please / Thank you / You're welcome / Sorry / Excuse me

---

## 14. Telling Time *(Unit 10)*

o'clock · quarter past · half past · quarter to

Example: It is **half past seven**. Class starts at **quarter to eight**.

# COMMON IRREGULAR WORDS

[Illustration: A small flat-icon illustration of a puzzle piece that doesn't quite fit a pattern, symbolizing "irregular" words that break the usual rules, teal and gold color accents.]

Most English words follow regular patterns (add **-s** for plurals, add **-s/-es** for verbs). But some very common words are **irregular** — they change in a special way. Learn these well, because you will use them often.

---

## Irregular Plural Nouns

| Singular (one) | Plural (many) |
|---|---|
| child | children |
| man | men |
| woman | women |
| person | people |
| foot | feet |
| tooth | teeth |

**Example sentences:**
- I have one **child**. My neighbor has three **children**.
- That **man** is a driver. Those **men** are drivers.

---

## The Irregular Verb "To Be"

The verb **"be"** does not follow the regular pattern. It changes completely depending on the subject:

| Subject | Present Form |
|---|---|
| I | am |
| you / we / they | are |
| he / she / it | is |

**Example:** I **am** a student. She **is** a nurse. They **are** my classmates.

---

## Irregular Third-Person Singular Verbs (Simple Present)

Most verbs simply add **-s** for he/she/it (study → studies is a spelling change, not truly irregular, but these below are worth memorizing carefully):

| Base Verb | He / She / It Form |
|---|---|
| go | goes |
| do | does |
| have | has |
| study | studies |
| watch | watches |

**Example:** I **go** to school by bus. She **goes** to school by bus.
I **have** a small family. He **has** a big family.

---

## Irregular Question Word Pairs (Easily Confused)

These words are not irregular verbs, but Somali learners often confuse them because they look or sound similar. Practice them carefully.

| Word | Meaning |
|---|---|
| this / these | near, one / near, many |
| that / those | far, one / far, many |
| his / her | belongs to a male / belongs to a female |
| there is / there are | one thing exists / many things exist |

---

## A Note for Teachers

At the Pre-A1 level, students are not expected to master past-tense irregular verbs (go → went, eat → ate, etc.). These will be introduced fully in **Basic English Level 2**. This section only covers the irregular forms students need for Level 1 material: the verb "to be," irregular plurals, and irregular third-person singular verbs.

# SKC TEACHER'S RESOURCE GUIDE

## Section 1: Teaching Tips

[Illustration: A confident SKC teacher standing in front of a class of adult Somali students, holding this teacher's guide, whiteboard behind covered in simple diagrams, warm and professional illustration style.]

This guide accompanies **Basic English Level 1: Survival English — Student Book**. It is intended for SKC instructors teaching true beginner, Pre-A1 adult learners.

---

### General Principles for Teaching Pre-A1 Adult Beginners

1. **Use English, but support with gesture, picture, and realia.** Most students have zero English background. Point, mime, and show real objects before translating.
2. **Repeat and recycle constantly.** New words must be seen and used at least 6–10 times across different activities before they are remembered.
3. **Keep instructions short.** Use the classroom command vocabulary from Unit 1 consistently every day: "Listen," "Look," "Repeat," "Sit down," "Stand up." Students will learn these commands quickly if you use them every lesson.
4. **Respect adult dignity.** These are adult learners, many with families, jobs, and life experience. Avoid childish tone; keep materials respectful and practical.
5. **Build confidence before accuracy.** At this level, successful communication matters more than perfect grammar. Praise attempts to speak, then gently correct.
6. **Use pair and group work often.** Adult learners often feel shy speaking in front of the whole class. Pair work lowers anxiety and increases speaking time per student.
7. **Connect every lesson to real life.** Every unit in this book is built around real Somali contexts — the market, the mosque, the bus, the family. Always link classroom English back to what students will actually do outside class.
8. **Check understanding, don't assume it.** Ask students to demonstrate (point, act, answer) rather than just asking "Do you understand?" — many students will say yes to be polite even if they are unsure.

---

### Managing a Mixed-Ability Classroom

- Pair a stronger student with a developing student for peer support during pair work.
- Provide the "⭐ Speaking Challenge" and "⭐ Extension Activity" items in each unit to keep faster learners engaged while others finish core tasks.
- Allow extra repetition time for writing tasks; tracing and copying exercises can be extended for students who need more handwriting practice.

---

### Using the Icons and Structure

Each unit follows the same 17-part structure (see "How to Use This Book" in the Front Matter). Teach students the icon system in Unit 1 so that by Unit 3 they can navigate the book independently and know what to expect in each section.

---

### Suggested Weekly Pacing

At 3 classroom sessions per week (90 minutes each), one unit typically takes **one week** to complete fully, including the Mini Assessment. This pacing allows:

- 20 units × 1 week = 20 weeks of instruction
- Plus 1 week for the Midterm Exam (after Unit 10)
- Plus 1 week for the Final Exam (after Unit 20)
- **Total: approximately 22 weeks**, suitable for a single college term or two shorter terms.

---

### Assessment Philosophy

- **Mini Assessments** (end of each unit) are low-stakes checks for understanding — use them to identify students who need more support before moving on.
- **Midterm and Final Exams** are formal assessments covering listening, reading, grammar, writing, and speaking equally (20 marks each), reflecting the four-skills, competency-based philosophy of the SKC curriculum.
- **Speaking assessment** should always be done individually or in small groups using the provided rubric, not as a large-group activity, to ensure fair and accurate evaluation.

# SKC TEACHER'S RESOURCE GUIDE

## Section 4: Class Activities Bank

[Illustration: A lively classroom scene with small groups of adult students playing different games simultaneously — one group with bingo cards, one with matching cards, one doing a role play — energetic, colorful flat-vector illustration.]

These general-purpose activities work with the vocabulary of **any** unit in this book. Use them to add variety beyond the specific games already listed in each unit.

---

### Matching Activities
- **Word-Picture Match:** Prepare word cards and picture cards for the unit's vocabulary. Students match them individually or in pairs.
- **Memory Match (Pelmanism):** Lay word and picture cards face-down in a grid. Students flip two at a time to find matching pairs.

### Coloring & Drawing Activities
- **Color and Label:** Students color a black-and-white picture sheet related to the unit theme, then label the parts in English.
- **Draw and Describe:** Students draw a simple picture (their family, house, or town) and write 3–5 labeled sentences about it.

### Circle/Choice Activities
- **Circle the Answer:** Give short sentences with two word choices; students circle the correct one.
- **True or False Race:** Read statements aloud; students hold up a "True" or "False" card as fast as they can.

### Pair and Group Work
- **Information Gap:** Give Student A and Student B different halves of information (e.g., a schedule); they ask each other questions to complete the full picture.
- **Interview Chains:** Students interview 3–5 classmates using unit questions, then report back to the class.

### Role Play
- **Real-Life Role Play:** Assign roles from the unit's conversation (teacher/student, shopkeeper/customer, vendor/customer) and have pairs perform for the class.
- **Switch Roles:** After one round, students swap roles and repeat with new information.

### Memory Games
- **Memory Chain Circle:** "In my bag, I have a pen." Next student repeats and adds one more item.
- **What's Missing?** Show 8–10 objects/pictures, remove one while students close their eyes, and ask them to identify what is missing.

### Find the Picture
- **Picture Hunt:** Hide vocabulary picture cards around the room; students find them and say the word aloud.
- **I Spy:** "I spy something red" — classmates guess the object.

### Word Search & Crossword
- Create simple word searches and crosswords using each unit's vocabulary list (found at the end of every unit) as your word bank.

### Speaking Challenges
- **One-Minute Talk:** Student speaks for one minute on a unit topic without stopping.
- **Question Relay:** Students stand in a line; each asks the next student a unit-related question in turn.

### Whole-Class Games
- **Bingo:** Create simple 9-square bingo cards from unit vocabulary; call out words or definitions.
- **Board Race:** Divide the class into teams; students race to write vocabulary words on the board correctly.
- **Charades:** Students act out a word (job, weather, activity) without speaking; classmates guess.

### Classroom Management Tips for Games
- Keep games under 10 minutes to maintain energy and focus.
- Always model the game once before starting.
- Use games to review previous units' vocabulary, not just the current unit — this builds long-term retention.

## Section 5: Extension Activities

[Illustration: A confident adult student raising a hand eagerly with a "star" badge on their notebook, symbolizing advanced or fast-finishing learners ready for extra challenges, bright and encouraging flat-vector illustration.]

Use these activities for students who finish core unit tasks early, or for classes that move faster than average. Each extension activity is designed to stretch the same unit content without introducing material beyond Level 1's scope.

---

### General Extension Strategies (Any Unit)

1. **Sentence Expansion:** Take a simple sentence from the unit ("I am a student.") and add one more detail each time ("I am a student at SKC." → "I am a student at SKC, and I study English.").
2. **Mini-Dialogue Writing:** Ask fast finishers to write a new 6–8 line dialogue using the unit's vocabulary and grammar point, then perform it for the class.
3. **Teach a Partner:** Fast finishers explain a vocabulary word or grammar rule to a classmate who is still working — this reinforces their own understanding.
4. **Illustrate a Word Wall:** Students draw and label extra vocabulary related to the unit theme that wasn't in the official list (e.g., extra foods, extra jobs).
5. **Journal Entry:** Write a 5-sentence journal entry connecting the unit topic to their own life.

---

### Unit-Specific Extension Ideas

- **Unit 1–2 (Greetings/Introductions):** Practice greeting in three different registers — very formal, neutral, and casual with a friend.
- **Unit 3 (Alphabet):** Create an "alphabet story" — one sentence per letter, e.g., "A is for apple. B is for book."
- **Unit 4 (Numbers):** Practice simple addition/subtraction word problems using classroom objects.
- **Unit 5 (Classroom Objects):** Design and label a "dream classroom" poster with 15+ objects.
- **Unit 6 (Family):** Create an extended family tree including cousins, in-laws, and write two sentences about each person.
- **Unit 7 (Colors):** Mix primary colors conceptually (red + yellow = orange) and describe combinations in English.
- **Unit 8 (Food):** Write a full-day meal plan (breakfast, lunch, dinner) using at least 10 food words.
- **Unit 9 (Days/Months):** Create a personal event calendar noting birthdays and holidays for the year.
- **Unit 10 (Time):** Design a complete daily schedule with 8–10 time-stamped activities.
- **Unit 11 (House):** Draw a two-story dream house and label 15+ rooms/furniture items.
- **Unit 12 (School):** Design an ideal school timetable with 6 subjects across 5 days.
- **Unit 13 (Community):** Draw a full neighborhood map with 10 locations and directions between them.
- **Unit 14 (Jobs):** Research (or ask a family member about) a job not covered in the unit and present it to the class.
- **Unit 15 (Shopping):** Role-play a shopping trip involving three items and a negotiated discount.
- **Unit 16 (Market):** Write a full shopping dialogue involving bargaining for five different items.
- **Unit 17 (Transport):** Compare the cost and time of three different transport options for the same journey.
- **Unit 18 (Weather):** Keep a one-week weather diary with a sentence for each day.
- **Unit 19 (Review):** Create a full quiz (10 questions) for classmates using vocabulary from at least 8 units.
- **Unit 20 (Final Project):** Add an additional section to the final project poster titled "My Advice for New Students" or "My Five-Year Plan."

---

### Extension for Advanced Speaking Practice

For students who are progressing quickly, encourage:
- Speaking for longer periods (2–3 minutes instead of 1 minute) without notes.
- Answering unexpected follow-up questions from the teacher.
- Leading pair-work activities as a "peer helper" for classmates who need more support.
